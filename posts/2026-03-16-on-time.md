The only thing you truly possess is time.

Not money, not property, not reputation — those can be lost, stolen, or given away and recovered. Time is different. It flows in one direction only, it cannot be stored, and once spent, no amount of wealth or cleverness buys it back. Every other resource is renewable. Time is not.

And yet we have it exactly backwards. We say *time is money*, as though time were merely an instrument for producing something more valuable. It is precisely the opposite. Money is what other people give you in exchange for your time. When you work, you are not converting time into money — you are selling it. You are trading irreplaceable fragments of your life for a token that society has agreed to accept as a store of value.

This is not a modern condition. It is the oldest bargain in human history. Before there were salaries or stock options or quarterly reviews, there was the same fundamental exchange: you spend part of your finite existence doing things you would rather not do — hunting, planting, hauling stone, digging coal — in order to secure the conditions that make the rest of life possible. The form changes across the centuries. The substance does not.

What has changed is abstraction, and here money reveals its genuine genius. Before currency, you traded time directly for goods — and only for whatever the other party happened to have and was willing to swap. Money dissolves that constraint. It is *fungible*: the hours you sold to one person can be redeemed from an entirely different person, for an entirely different thing, at an entirely different time. That is not a trivial trick. It is the mechanism that made civilization possible, decoupling the moment of earning from the moment of spending, and freeing exchange from the tyranny of coincident wants. Money is a remarkable abstraction. The problem has never been the abstraction itself.

Which brings us to a verse that is almost universally misquoted. People say *money is the root of all evil*. That is not what 1 Timothy 6:10 says. What it says is that *the love of money is the root of all evil*. The distinction is not subtle — it is the entire point. The indictment falls on a disposition, not an instrument. Money is neutral; it is the venality of those who pursue it as an end rather than a means that corrupts. The text understood something that the misquote obscures: the abstraction is innocent. The hunger for it, untethered from any purpose beyond accumulation, is where the rot sets in.

This should clarify what it means to spend money. When you buy something, you are not really spending money — you are spending the portion of your life you exchanged to earn it. The price of anything is ultimately denominated in hours: your hours, extracted from the only life you have. Whether that trade is worth making is a question worth taking seriously.

The deeper implication is this: how you spend your time is the most consequential decision you make, because it *is* your life, not a preparation for it. Money is downstream of time. It is what remains after the transaction — useful, fungible, and entirely beside the point if you have spent the irreplaceable thing to get it. What you do with the hours that no one has yet bought from you, that is the question that matters.

Time is not money. Money is the shadow that time casts when it passes through the economy. Spend it accordingly.
