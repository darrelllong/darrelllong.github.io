The only thing you truly possess is time.

Not money, not property, not reputation — those can be lost, stolen, or given away and recovered. Time is different. It flows in one direction only, it cannot be stored, and once spent, no amount of wealth or cleverness buys it back. Every other resource is renewable. Time is not.

And yet we have it exactly backwards. We say *time is money*, as though time were merely an instrument for producing something more valuable. It is precisely the opposite. Money is what other people give you in exchange for your time. When you work, you are not converting time into money — you are selling it. You are trading irreplaceable fragments of your life for a fungible token that society has agreed to accept as a store of value.

This is not a modern condition. It is the oldest bargain in human history. Before there were salaries or stock options or quarterly reviews, there was the same fundamental exchange: you spend part of your finite existence doing things you would rather not do — hunting, planting, hauling stone, digging coal — in order to secure the conditions that make the rest of life possible. The form changes across the centuries. The substance does not.

What has changed is abstraction. The farmer who broke his back in a field could at least see the grain. The miner emerging from the shaft could hold the ore. Today most of us trade our hours for numbers that move between accounts — representations of representations, value encoded in signals that exist only because we collectively agree they do. Money is a social fiction, and a useful one, but it is nothing more than a ledger entry memorializing the time you gave to someone else.

This should clarify what it means to spend money. When you buy something, you are not really spending money. You are spending the portion of your life you exchanged to earn it. The price tag on anything is ultimately denominated in hours — your hours, extracted from the only life you have. Whether that trade is worth making is a question worth taking seriously.

The deeper implication is this: how you spend your time is the most consequential decision you make, because it *is* your life, not a preparation for it. Money is downstream of time. It is what remains after the transaction. What you do with the time that no one has yet bought from you — that is the question that matters.

Time is not money. Money is the shadow that time casts when it passes through the economy. Spend it accordingly.
