Wesley Mackey earned his Ph.D. in Computer Science from the University of Manitoba. He hated the cold, which may explain the rest of his career: first to Florida International University in the early 1980s, then to California. By the early 1990s he was at UC Santa Cruz, where he would stay for roughly thirty years as a lecturer in Computer Science.

Over three decades he taught the courses that every CS major had to get through: Fundamentals of Compiler Design (CS 104A/B at the undergraduate level, CS 204 for graduate students), Comparative Programming Languages (CS 112), Advanced Programming. Those courses were originally mine. I had designed and taught both the compilers sequence and the programming languages course before I handed them to Mackey when I moved over to teaching the operating systems course. When CS later merged with Computer Engineering to become CSE, the course numbers were renumbered, but the material carried through.

Mackey worked from my notes and made those courses his own. In the compilers sequence, he had the students write a stripped-down C compiler — all the way through to code generation — in a language called C-- (that was my fault too). The CS 112 setup where students learned a new programming language every two weeks was also my design, but what Mackey did with both courses was pure Mackey. He wrote his own grading scripts in Perl. When UCSC used narrative evaluations instead of letter grades, he wrote Perl scripts to generate those too.

He loved programming, he loved languages, and he loved teaching people about both. He was patient with questions and accessible outside of class. He kept using older languages and tools that some students found dated, but he was teaching fundamentals. In 2007-08, UCSC gave him the Excellence in Teaching Award, a campus-wide honor based on student nominations. Students from different decades would compare notes and realize they'd all had the same experience — the rigor, the knowledge, the willingness to help. "The man's just casually been a legend for 30+ years," one student wrote.

Mackey was also a Latinist, genuinely interested in Roman history, which is not something you run into much in a CS department. I discovered this myself, somewhat accidentally. About twenty years ago I came back from a sabbatical in England — I was at Lancaster University, and I had worked through the entire *History of Rome* podcast walking to and around campus. When I walked around town I tried to listen to the people, but they all talked funny and were hard to understand. When I got back to Santa Cruz I mentioned it to Mackey. He lit up. That's when I learned he was a Latinist, because a man who is genuinely excited to learn about a podcast on Roman history turns out to be a man who reads Latin. His email came from fatoprofugus.soe.ucsc.edu rather than a standard @ucsc.edu address. Students noticed and puzzled over it. The phrase is from the second line of Virgil's *Aeneid*: *fato profugus*, "driven by fate" — Virgil's description of Aeneas, who fled the ruins of Troy and wandered the Mediterranean until he reached Italy. A Canadian who fled the cold for Miami and then Santa Cruz could do worse for a motto.

He had essentially no internet presence — no faculty webpage, no LinkedIn, no social media, no published photo that anyone could find. Students on Reddit would ask if anyone had a picture of him, and nobody did.

Mackey shows up in the [Songs of our People](https://github.com/darrelllong/Songs-of-our-People), a collection of CS parodies of Beatles songs. In "Code in C" (to the tune of "Let It Be"), the lyric goes: "When my code has tons of trouble, Wesley Mackey comes to me, speaking words of wisdom: Code in C."

He retired in 2022 after about twenty-nine years at UCSC. When someone posted the news on a student forum, the reply was: "End of an era."

I knew Wesley Mackey. He was a good man who cared about his students, and they were lucky to have him.
